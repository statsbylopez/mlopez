Part I: Randomness of regular season competition

One of the most humbling aspects of being a sports fan is that the better team doesn’t always win. It could happen for a few reasons. The better team might get unlucky, whether it’s a missed foul call or bad bounce. The better team might play poorly, missing open three-pointers, field goals, or easy saves. Or the “better”” team might simply be so close in terms of talent to the “worse” team that any number of plays, shots, or referee decisions could play into the game’s outcome, actions that, by and large, are largely noise and not indicative of any particular skill or talent.

The impact of make-up calls is probably bigger than you think

One of the more reliable indicators of which NHL team is likely to get the next power play has little to do with score or style of play. Instead, it’s been shown repeatedly (Ex 1, Ex 2) that referees call a substantially higher number of make-up penalties than would otherwise be expected in order to maintain an overall even number of violations on each team. Call it a biased impartiality – in order to appear impartial by game’s end, ref’s let previous decisions drive future ones.
